Strategy

Learn the key strategies of law school admissions, to ensure your best foot is put forward!

Step 1: Build Your Foundation and Study

>18 Weeks from Application Submission

Start by learning the basics of the law school admissions process. Read common terminology in the Resource Library page. Understand what the LSAT is, how law schools admit students, and what you'll have to do to prepare. At this stage, it is time to begin your serious LSAT preparation. It is never too early to start studying for the LSAT, and it is a common myth to think it is.

NOTE:

It is best to apply to law schools as early in the cycle as possible, which is from Sept-Nov of any given year. As the cycle continues, the number of reserved seats fill up and your chance of admission becomes more slim. Plan your application around a submission between Sept-Nov.

Step 2: Preparing The Strong Materials

<18 Weeks from Application Submission

At this phase, start to gather your admissions materials. Start with the Letters of Recommendation. Most applications require two, one of which should come from a collegiate professor. Then, begin writing your personal statement, resume, and addenda where needed. Poor application materials will hurt you, and this part of an application should be easy points! There is plenty of time to produce, proofread and perfect your materials.

Step 3: Take the LSAT

12 Weeks from Application Submission

It is time to take the LSAT! With 12 weeks before your first application, it is the perfect time to sign up to take your first LSAT. Your score will be made available with time to spare, and if something goes wrong, you will still have time for a retake. Stay focused, balance your time well, and stay confident. You spent several weeks training!

Step 4: Curate a School List

4 Weeks from Application Submission

Now is the time to make a list of schools you would love to attend. You should have your application materials, LSAT score, GPA, and a decent idea of where you would like to attend geographically. Using these personal metrics, you will pick 12 schools you would like to attend, split evenly into safety, target, and reach schools. This method grants you the best chance at getting into a good school while being mindful of application costs.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Waiting too long to begin LSAT preparation
  • Applying without researching schools carefully
  • Submitting poorly written materials
  • Applying to too many/too few schools
  • Ignoring a strict, self-imposed deadline
  • Using a weak application strategy
