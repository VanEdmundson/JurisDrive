Admissions Topics

The LSAT stands for the Law School Admission Test It is a standardized test used by law schools to gauge how well potential students can handle the difficulty of law school. The test specializes in assessing your reading comprehension, reasoning and analytical skills. The LSAT has become a "make or break" factor in admissions, usually being the biggest factor for deciding what law schools an individual can get in to. In August of 2024, the LSAT removed one of its core sections, called "Logic Games". Moving forward, the test is comprised of two Logical Reasoning sections, one Reading Comprehension section, and one unscored section called the "Experimental" section. Each section contains roughly 26 questions.

Application materials usually include a personal statement, resume, transcripts, letters of recommendation, and optional essays or addenda. Law schools typically have additional standards for their applicant submissions.

For example, Yale Law School is known for having the "Yale 250", a 250 word essay about anything from your academic, extracurricular, or professional work that is of particular interest to you.

Some of the top-ranked law schools require interviews. Interviews give schools another way to evaluate your communication skills, professionalism, and interests.

It is important to prepare for the interview as best as you can, though it is important to keep in mind that even the best interview does not grant you admission. Additionally, a mediocre interview does not always produce a rejection.

Terminology

An application is the culimnation of materials and documents you send to a law school. It typically includes your LSAT score, collegiate transcripts, a resume, a personal statement, recommendation letters, and other required documents. Schools can change what is required for an admission into their school, but these are the typical core application materials.

The law school rankings are posted yearly by USNWR (U.S News & World Report). There are roughly 198 ABA (American Bar Association) accredited law schools in the United States. Schools are typically ranked by their outcomes and other factors. It is important to keep in mind that ranking is somewhat subjective.

A personal statement is a common application material, usually a double-spaced, two-page essay personally written. Applicants usually write about a personal experience they've had, or a life-story they would like to share. The writing is analyzed by admission officers to see if the applicant is fit for their school.

A resume is a common application material that summarizes an applicant's education, work experience, skillsets, and accomplishments. A great resume can make you stand out among other applicants.

A letter of recommendation, or LoR, is a written evaluation from anyone who can speak to an applicant's abilities and accomplishments. Two LoR's are typically required, and it is imperative to have one of them coming from your undergrad experience, like a professor.

An addendum is a short explanation attached to an application to clarify something unusual in your life, such as a gap in education, a low grade period, or a character and fitness issue.

These terms refer to years in law school. A 1L is a first-year law student, a 2L is a second-year law student, and a 3L is a third-year law student.

JD stands for Juris Doctor. It is the standard law degree earned in the United States before taking the bar exam at the end of law school and becoming a licensed attorney.

The bar exam is the exam that law graduates must pass in order to practice law in a state. This test is usually taken and passed at the end of your law school. It is important to analyze a law school's bar passage rate before attending.

Early Decision is a type of application option where an applicant agrees to attend a school if admitted, making it a binding commitment. This can help a school see that an applicant is serious about attending, and can assist with an admission.

A waitlist can be a school's response to an applicant, which means a school has not denied or accepted the applicant. The applicant may still be admitted later if space becomes available. Essentially, it is a school saying they may or may not admit an applicant, depending on how the cycle goes.
